# The commercial‑real‑estate crisis since 2020: effects on fundamentals, financing risk, exposure, responses, and policy options

## Executive summary
Since 2020 office fundamentals deteriorated sharply and financing stress concentrated in office loans, producing large near‑term refinancing needs and higher distress in CMBS pools and bank portfolios. U.S. office vacancy and availability more than doubled in many markets, transaction‑based prices and values fell, cap rates rose, and a concentrated “debt‑maturity wall” in 2024–2027 created refinancing and special‑servicing pressures that vary materially by metro, property class, lender type and borrower profile. Policy and private responses to date include loan extensions and restructurings, targeted conversion finance and tax incentives, and supervisory stress tests; remaining risks depend heavily on future interest‑rate paths and whether rents/absorption continue to stabilize or re‑weaken. [1], [7], [13]

## Market fundamentals (vacancy, rents, leasing, transactions, valuations, cap rates)
- Vacancy and leasing. Nationally, U.S. direct office vacancy exceeded 20% in mid‑2025 with total vacancy above 22% (Q2 2025), and net absorption remained negative through 2024 before returning to positive quarterly flows in 2025–2026 in some quarters, indicating uneven recovery across metros and property tiers [1], [5], [3]. Leasing volumes remained below pre‑pandemic norms (leasing in 2022–2024 running well under 2019 averages) even as large gateway transactions and renewals boosted activity in select CBDs (Manhattan, some downtowns) [4], [16].
- Metro and class dispersion. Metro outcomes are divergent: San Francisco, Dallas and Chicago reported among the highest vacancy readings in mid‑2024, while New York City’s vacancy rose materially from early‑2020 levels and 5‑Star inventory showed both rising vacancy and larger high‑end inventory increases; some Sunbelt metros saw stronger rent growth and lower declines in demand [2], [3], [15].
- Prices, values and cap rates. Transaction‑based price indices and property valuations fell in 2022–2023 and showed stabilization into 2024–2026, but office values declined most of all property types and cap rates rose from the compressed pre‑2022 lows; Q4‑2025 metro office cap rates ranged across New York (~6.9%) to Chicago (~10.1%), reflecting risk dispersion [21], [14].

(Notes: national vacancy/absorption and metro figures draw on JLL, CBRE, Avison Young and local Comptroller analyses; price and cap‑rate observations come from transaction indices and market cap‑rate surveys) [1], [3], [4], [14].

## Financing stress: CMBS, bank loans, delinquencies, maturities and refinancing outcomes
- CMBS and special servicing. CMBS office delinquency reached historic highs in early‑2026, driven in part by a handful of very large NYC office loans, and CMBS special‑servicing rates for offices rose into mid‑teens territory in late 2024–early 2026 before some modest easing; overall CMBS delinquency also rose versus 2023 levels [6], [8].
- Bank portfolios and aggregate delinquencies. Aggregate commercial‑mortgage delinquency rates ticked higher into 2026, though bank‑book CRE delinquency rates (excluding farmland) remained low‑single‑digits on many series; banks hold roughly half of outstanding CRE debt, concentrating exposure in regional and smaller institutions [9], [7].
- Maturities and refinancing outcomes. A multi‑trillion dollar cohort of CRE loans faced maturities through 2026–2027; 2026 alone carried an unusually large “hard” CMBS maturity schedule (tens of billions), with low debt‑yield loans concentrated among office, retail and some multifamily maturities. Historical pay‑off rates varied: for many office loans scheduled to mature in 2024–2025 roughly half to three‑quarters paid off on time, but unresolved maturities often showed very low payoff recovery and high non‑paying rates, with debt yields markedly lower for unresolved payoffs [11], [12], [10], [9].

Together, these forces produced a differentiated outcome: elevated CMBS office delinquencies and special servicing concentrated on legacy, low‑debt‑yield loans and large CBD assets, while many bank portfolios showed limited aggregate delinquencies but meaningful concentration risk in regional issuers and in banks with high CRE shares [6], [9], [12].

## Who and what is most exposed
- Property types and borrowers. The office sector accounts for the lion’s share of new distress since 2021, and office properties dominate unresolved delinquency and special‑servicing volumes; other sectors (industrial, multifamily) have been far less stressed in aggregate [11], [14].
- Lenders and investors. Banks (especially regional and smaller institutions) hold about half of CRE debt and thus carry concentrated refinancing risk; CMBS investors face elevated delinquencies and special servicing in office portfolios. Listed REIT exposure to office is relatively small versus the size of the market, implying losses concentrate outside listed equity markets [9], [12].
- Geography and asset class slices. Metros with high vacancy (San Francisco, Dallas, Chicago, parts of Manhattan/NYC) and markets with large supply pipelines or elevated sublet availability show greater stress; trophy and large CBD loans generated outsized CMBS distress due to size and legacy underwriting [2], [3], [15], [1].

## Market responses and workout strategies
- Lender/servicer tactics. Common private responses included loan extensions, restructurings, special servicing interventions and selective distress sales; many 2023 maturities were extended in an “extend‑and‑pretend” pattern, while a material share of CMBS office loans entered special servicing [11], [6].
- Conversions and public‑sector support. Conversion finance and tax incentives have been deployed or proposed to repurpose office stock: federal/state programs and local incentives (examples include HUD‑supported conversions and a NYC tax‑exemption program to incentivize non‑residential→residential conversion) support targeted reuse where feasible, though conversion feasibility is limited to a small share of overall office stock in many cities [17], [18], [19], [18].
- Municipal fiscal and transaction responses. Some municipalities have used tax‑policy adjustments or paused reassessment/tax projections to blunt immediate fiscal impacts, while real‑estate transfer taxes (e.g., Measure ULA in Los Angeles) materially affected transaction volumes in certain jurisdictions [19], [24].

## Near‑term scenarios and systemic risks
Federal and supervisory scenarios test sharp CRE price declines (Fed proposed severely adverse scenarios include CRE price shocks near 39–40%) and large macro stress paths; under such scenarios bank capital and liquidity strains concentrate in banks with high CRE shares, and CMBS losses concentrate in office‑heavy pools [25], [13]. The Central Bank of Ireland’s scenario analysis illustrates how vacancy trajectories can materially diverge (benign vs severe) — underscoring sensitivity to macro and rate paths [20]. Key systemic risk is therefore concentrated: a severe macro/rate shock that causes another leg down in office rents/values would amplify CMBS delinquencies and stress regionally concentrated bank lenders, whereas a stabilizing rates/rent recovery would materially reduce expected losses and improve refinance/payoff rates [25], [13], [11].

## Evidence gaps
- Loan‑level borrower credit profiles and detailed lender counterparty maps (beyond aggregate bank/CMBS splits) are not provided in the reviewed evidence.
- Full metro‑by‑metro quantitative transition‑feasibility studies for office‑to‑residential conversions (detailed cost/value break‑evens) are limited in the available material.
- Up‑to‑date municipal fiscal impact tables that tie property‑value declines to explicit budget outcomes for a broad set of cities are sparse outside select city reports.

## Policy and private‑sector options (evidence‑based)
- For lenders/servicers: prioritize targeted restructurings for viable assets, accelerate distinction between structurally impaired loans and transitory‑stress loans, and increase transparency around debt‑yields and cash‑flow remediation (supported by CMBS and bank servicing data) to improve market pricing and limit value destruction [9], [6].
- For municipalities and federal policymakers: deploy targeted conversion finance and tax‑incentives where market feasibility exists; use federal programs (HUD financing models) and local tax‑exemptions to crowd‑in conversion/reuse while avoiding across‑the‑board property‑tax relief that masks structural decline [17], [18], [19].
- For supervisors: continue stress testing with severe CRE price scenarios and require banks with concentrated CRE exposure to hold extra capital/liquidity buffers or limit new CRE growth until refinancing windows pass [25], [13].
