# Beyond Vector Search: How Cross-Encoder Reranking Transforms RAG Pipelines

Vector search has become the foundation of modern retrieval-augmented generation, but it carries a fundamental limitation: it treats queries and documents as independent entities. [uncited] A reranker, also known as a cross-encoder, is a model that takes a user's query and a single retrieved document and outputs a relevance score to determine how relevant that specific document is to that specific query. [1] This seemingly simple capability unlocks substantial improvements in RAG system performance.

The power of reranking lies in its two-stage architecture. Two-stage retrieval uses a fast and efficient retriever to scan an entire database and pull a large set of potentially relevant documents (e.g., the top 50) in Stage 1, prioritizing speed over accuracy, followed by Stage 2 where a reranker carefully examines each of those documents alongside the user's query and assigns a precise relevance score. [1] This design elegantly solves a computational constraint: rerankers are computationally intensive and slow, while initial retrievers are fast and efficient, so using a reranker on a full database would be far too slow and costly; instead, a fast retriever creates a smaller, more manageable list for the reranker to work on. [1]

What makes cross-encoders fundamentally different from vector search? A cross-encoder processes query and document simultaneously, allowing it to understand how specific words in the query interact with specific words in the document, unlike a standard embedding model (bi-encoder) which calculates the query and document separately. [2] Cross-encoders compute relevance scores in real time by jointly encoding the query and document as a single input, enabling full self-attention across all tokens in both the query and the document at every layer of the transformer, allowing the model to capture fine-grained semantic interactions. [3]

The empirical gains are substantial. Cross-encoder reranking improves RAG accuracy by 40% according to research, with improvements varying by query type: fact lookup (+18%), multi-hop (+47%), complex (+52%), and ambiguous queries (+41%). [4] MIT researchers published a comprehensive study analyzing the impact of cross-encoder reranking on RAG system performance, finding consistent improvements across diverse datasets and query types, with two-stage retrieval with cross-encoder reranking significantly outperforming single-stage vector search. [5] Across multiple benchmarks, cross-encoder reranking improves RAG accuracy: MS MARCO (+42.0%), Natural Questions (+38.4%), HotpotQA (+42.1%), FEVER (+19.4%), with an average improvement of +33.1%. [5]

Cohere Rerank 4.0 exemplifies modern reranking capabilities. Cohere Rerank improves RAG by reading both the query and document together (cross-encoding), producing highly precise semantic relevance scores, resulting in more accurate search results, more grounded responses in RAG pipelines, lower generative model usage reducing cost, and higher trust and quality across enterprise workloads. [6] The model comes in two versions: Rerank 4.0 Fast (best balance of speed and accuracy with higher latency than Cohere Rerank 3.5 but significantly higher accuracy) and Rerank 4.0 Pro (highest accuracy across all benchmarks, excelling at complex reasoning-heavy domain-specific retrieval). [7] [6]

The tradeoff is real but manageable. In a typical two-stage retrieval system, a bi-encoder retrieves the top 100 candidates from the full corpus in approximately 5 milliseconds, while a cross-encoder rescores those 100 candidates in approximately 50 milliseconds. [8] For production systems, cross-encoders achieve high accuracy (NDCG@10: 0.85+) with medium latency (200ms-2s) and low cost ($0.025-0.050 per million tokens), making them ideal for production search and RAG. [9]

Reranking is recommended for any RAG agent use case, with customers typically seeing massive quality gains when their current systems find the right answer somewhere in the top 50 results from retrieval but struggle to surface it within the top 10. [10] For applications where accuracy is non-negotiable (aviation safety, medical documentation, legal research), the investment in reranking pays dividends in user trust and system reliability. [11]

# The Power of Cross-Encoder Reranking: Elevating RAG Beyond Vector Search

[uncited] Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has become central to building AI systems that ground responses in real data. Yet a critical bottleneck remains: how do you ensure the language model receives the *most relevant* information from your knowledge base? Vector search alone often falls short. Cross-encoder rerankers like Cohere Rerank offer a proven solution—a second stage that dramatically improves what matters most: precision.

## Why Vector Search Alone Isn't Enough

[1] Dense retrieval models struggle with more complex data and questions due to the simplicity of the method, as document vectors contain the meaning of text in a compressed representation—typically 768-1536 dimension vectors—which often results in loss of information because information is compressed into a single vector. [2] A bi-encoder can be thought of as a fingerprint scanner that can tell you that two things share the same general shape and pattern but cannot tell you whether a specific detail is present, while a cross-encoder is like a forensic analyst who examines every feature of both prints side by side, looking for specific matches and mismatches.

## How Cross-Encoders Work Differently

[3] Cross-encoders compute relevance scores in real time by jointly encoding the query and document as a single input, enabling full self-attention across all tokens in both the query and the document at every layer of the transformer, allowing the model to capture fine-grained semantic interactions. [4] A bi-encoder cannot reason about the relationship between specific words in the query and specific words in the document because it just sees token overlap in the compressed vectors, while a cross-encoder reads the query and document together and can catch subtle mismatches.

## Measurable Improvements Across Benchmarks

[5] Cross-encoder reranking improves retrieval quality across multiple benchmarks: MS MARCO improved from 37.2% to 52.8% (+42.0%), Natural Questions from 45.6% to 63.1% (+38.4%), HotpotQA from 41.3% to 58.7% (+42.1%), and FEVER from 68.2% to 81.4% (+19.4%), with an average improvement of +33.1%. [5] Reranking effectiveness varies by query type: fact lookup queries show +18% improvement, multi-hop queries show +47% improvement, complex queries show +52% improvement, and ambiguous queries show +41% improvement.

[6] Two-stage retrieval pipelines use Stage 1 for retrieval (recall) to cast a wide net and ensure the right document is somewhere in the pile, and Stage 2 for reranking (precision) to sort the pile using a cross-encoder to read the top candidates and push the best ones to the top. [1] By applying Cohere Rerank after the first-stage retrieval, the RAG orchestration can gain the benefits of both approaches: while first-stage retrieval helps capture relevant items based on proximity matches within the vector space, reranking helps optimize search according to results by guaranteeing contextually relevant results are surfaced to the top.
